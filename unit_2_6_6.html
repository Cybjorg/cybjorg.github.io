So, when should technology be used in the classroom?

To answer this question, the teacher should always start with the learning outcomes and consider what they want the students to know and understand. This follows the principles of backward design (i.e., designing curriculum and activities based on the desired learning outcomes), which you will learn more about in the 3.5 Understanding by Design lesson.    

If technology can facilitate the learning process and enhance student learning, then you have your answer. Having a clear purpose or reason for using technology, is the key to leveraging the benefits of technology and avoiding its pitfalls.

Of course, there are times when technology can get in the way or serve as a distraction to the learning process. Teachers will need to work with students to manage their use of technology so that the focus remains on the learning target.

As we will discuss in future lessons, schools and teachers can play a powerful role helping students develop effective habits and approaches to their own learning. For example, students will need to learn how to manage their screen time, develop their media literacy skills, and become productive, digital citizens.

Ask yourself...

Some questions you can ask yourself as a teacher when deciding on the use of technology are:

  • What technology is available?
  • How reliable is the technology?
  • Can the learning outcomes be achieved without technology or with minimal technology?
  • Would using technology for the lesson increase engagement or be a distraction?
  • Would using technology allow students to participate at a more appropriate level?
  • Am I or my students overusing technology? (e.g., Is there an appropriate balance between screen and non-screen time?)  
  • Would using technology allow students to focus on bigger concepts and make more connections?
  • Will using this technology be safe? (e.g., Do students have to create an account and share private information?)
  • Will the technology overshadow the learning outcome?
