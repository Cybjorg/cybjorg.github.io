In Unit 5.4 - "Everything Feedback" we described the importance of word choice when communicating with students. Selecting the right words is a key component when communicating with parents that have expressed concern about our teaching style or question our practices as classroom teacher.

Here are some of the key points that relate to communicating with parents:

Scenario Communication Do's Communication Don'ts
Describing student behaviors 
  • Choose words that describe the actions and provide examples, such as "s/he has not turned in...
  • Use words such as "usually" or "rarely"
  • If the student behavior or actions are extreme, a comparison to the others can be appropriate. "s/he is the only student who was not able to submit an outline"
  • Do not use generalizations such as "s/he rarely turns in homework."
  • Do not use "absolute" words such as "always" or "never".
  • Do not use  comparisons if the action/behavior is not extreme.
  • Do not compare to specific students.
Tone of communication

Ensure the tone of your communication implies the parent is trying to solve the same problem(s) you are in helping the student to:

  • master the academic content;
  • expand their social and emotional skill set as they develop appropriate and sustainable relationships for positive personal growth.
  • Imply that you know more about the student than the parent, or use language that is telling the parent what they should be doing.
  • Do not come across as though you are defending yourself.
Comparing students
  • Use comparisons sparingly, however, at times they are helpful, for example, "Jin is one of only two students in my classes that was not able to complete the project."
  • Don't compare to specific students.
  • Don't ignore comparative facts that might help the parent realise this is a serious issue.
Professional writing
  • Use proper grammar and full sentences.
  • Ensure the length is sensible length.
  • Ensure the communication is focused on the issue at hand.
  • Do not use "educational" language the parent might not understand.
  • Do not use acronyms or abbreviations the parent might not know.
